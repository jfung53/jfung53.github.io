Which middle school demographics predict SHSAT test-taking rates?

R NYC DOE

Every October, 1/3 of all NYC 8th graders take the SHSAT admissions test to try for a seat at one of the city's eight Specialized High Schools (SHS). Racial categories have been well-discussed. Are there other demographics that intersect?

The answer: yes, definitely. A school's poverty rate predicts fewer test takers (p < 0.001).

Stats and methods

Described by some as elite, prestigious, and ultra-competitive, NYC's Specialized High Schools (SHS) are seen by some families as a launchpad into top universities. Whether or not any of this is true , the fact remains that Specialized High Schools are extremely selective and not very diverse .

 

The Specialized High Schools Admissions Test (SHSAT) is the only admissions method. All 8th graders are eligible to take the test, but test takers have a specific racial profile. There have been attempts to level the playing field so that the demographics of these schools mirror the city's demographics more closely, but these attempts have been met with contention for a variety of reasons and have yet to move the needle .

 

Race is well-studied when it comes to SHS admissions, but I was curious about how other demographics may intersect.

Methodology

I looked at the 2024 SHSAT admissions outcomes and the 2023-24 demographic snapshot of the 673 public middle schools in NYC. The demographics I analyzed, as recorded by the Department of Education: gender, poverty rate, english language learners (ELL), students with disabilities (SWD), and race (Asian, Black, White, Native American, Hispanic, and multi-racial).

 

To predict outcomes I fit a forward stepwise multiple regression model using the olsrr package in R.

Limitations

Some predictors could only be discerned at the middle school level, but data at the student level would be more meaningful.

 

Also, variations in economic hardship within a racial category can't be discerned with this data, so these results obscure the many lived experiences of individual students.

Results

Findings, correlation matrix, and linear regression plot.

A middle school's poverty rate shows the most significant effects on predicting how many students per middle school sit take the SHSAT.

 

Again, data at the student level would be more useful. Regardless, it's difficult to argue that admissions via SHSAT is inherently fair when there are multiple demographics that can predict test taking rates.
